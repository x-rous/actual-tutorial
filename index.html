Concept

What makes Actual Budget different?

Most budgeting apps are cloud services — your bank details and transaction history live on a company's servers. Actual is different: it's self-hosted, meaning your data lives on a server you control. No subscriptions, no company mining your financial life.

Actual is free and open-source. You can run it on your own computer, on a home server, or on a cheap provider like PikaPods for a few dollars a month. The app itself is a web interface that works in any browser — including your phone.

In Actual this looks like…

When you first open Actual, you create a "budget file." Everything — your accounts, transactions, categories, and reports — lives in that file. It syncs across your devices via your server. If you ever want to stop using Actual, you export your data and you own it completely.

Concept

What does a budgeting app actually do for you?

A budgeting app answers two questions that most people can't honestly answer without one: "Where did my money go this month?" and "Do I actually have enough to pay this bill?"

Actual does this by connecting all your accounts in one place, letting you categorize every transaction, and then showing you reports that reflect reality — not your best guesses.

In Actual this looks like…

You set up your bank accounts once. Then on a regular basis (daily, weekly, or whenever) you import new transactions and assign each one to a category like "Groceries" or "Rent." The budget page shows how your actual spending compares to your plan.

Two Budgeting Modes: Which One to Start With
Lesson 02 of 09 ⏱ 7 min

Two Budgeting Modes — Understanding the Difference

This is the most important lesson in the course. Actual offers two completely different philosophies for budgeting. Understanding both — before you touch any settings — will save you confusion for months.

🎯 By the end of this lesson you will…
Understand what Tracking Budget mode is and how it works
Understand what Envelope (zero-sum) mode is and how it works
Know exactly when to use each mode
Know how to switch between modes (it's easy and reversible)
Mode 1 — Recommended Starting Point

Tracking Budget: "I want to see where my money goes"

Tracking Budget works like a traditional spreadsheet budget. You set monthly targets for each category (Groceries: $400, Rent: $1,200…) and then track how your real spending compares. That's it.

You're forecasting what you expect to spend. You don't need to account for every dollar you own. At month-end, the budget doesn't carry over — you start fresh. If you overspend a category, you simply increase the budget number to acknowledge reality.

In Actual this looks like…

The budget page shows three columns per category: Budgeted (your target), Spent (what actually happened), and Balance (the difference). A green balance means under budget. Red means over. No money moves — you just adjust the number.

📍The Tracking Budget page — three columns per category. The summary at the top shows Projected Savings = budgeted income minus budgeted expenses.
👍
Why start here? Tracking mode has zero-pressure entry. You don't need to perfectly plan your finances from day one. Just set rough targets, import transactions, and observe. After 2–3 months you'll have real data about your habits — and that data is gold.
Mode 2 — For When You're Ready

Envelope Budget: "I want to control every dollar"

Envelope budgeting (also called zero-sum budgeting) is based on a single rule: every dollar you currently own must be assigned to a category. All of it. If you have $3,000 across your accounts, you allocate all $3,000 to categories until the "To Budget" counter reaches exactly zero.

The power comes from one constraint: when you overspend a category, you cannot simply increase the number. You must move money from another category to cover it. Want to spend an extra $50 on restaurants? You have to take $50 away from something else — entertainment, clothing, savings. Every spending decision has a visible trade-off.

In Actual this looks like…

The budget page shows a "To Budget" number at the top. You click each category and fill in amounts until To Budget = $0. If Eating Out goes over, you click that category's balance and choose "Transfer from another category." Leftover money in categories rolls forward to next month automatically — building up a cushion over time.

⚠️
Why not start here? Envelope mode is powerful but demands more from you. You need to check in regularly, resolve overspending actively, and think carefully about every allocation. It's the right tool once you understand your spending — but it can feel overwhelming on day one.

Concept

Categories: the buckets your money flows into

Every transaction in Actual must be assigned to a category. Categories are how Actual knows what your money was spent on — without them, you have a list of transactions with no meaning. The categories you create become the labels on all your reports and budget charts.

Actual lets you group categories — so "Groceries" and "Eating Out" can both sit inside a "Food" group. This keeps the budget page readable and lets you see group-level totals at a glance.

In Actual this looks like…

On the Budget page, the left column is a list of your category groups, each expandable to show the categories inside. You set a "Budgeted" amount next to each category. As transactions come in and are assigned to categories, the "Spent" and "Balance" columns update automatically.

Practical

Designing your categories — a starting framework

The right categories are personal, but here's a framework that works for most people. Start with these groups and refine over time. Fewer categories is better to start — you can always split later.

🔴 Fixed Essentials

Bills that are the same (or similar) every month and non-negotiable.

  • Rent or Mortgage
  • Insurance (health, car, home)
  • Internet & Phone
  • Electricity / Gas / Water
🟡 Variable Daily Spending

Spending that fluctuates but happens every month.

  • Groceries
  • Eating Out / Restaurants
  • Fuel / Transport
  • Personal Care & Clothing
🔵 Subscriptions

Recurring charges you may not notice day-to-day.

  • Streaming (Netflix, Spotify)
  • Software / Apps
  • Gym / Memberships
🟢 Savings Goals

Money you're deliberately setting aside.

  • Emergency Fund
  • Vacation
  • Car / Home Repair Fund
💡
Don't spend more than 10 minutes on categories before you have real transaction data. Set up something reasonable now, then refine it after your first import. The app makes it easy to rename, merge, and split categories later.
Concept

Setting targets: your monthly forecast

For each income category (like "Salary"), enter what you expect to receive this month. For each expense category, enter your spending target. These are forecasts, not contracts. Being wrong is fine — the goal is to get a baseline you can refine.

How to find good starting numbers: Open 2–3 months of bank statements and estimate your average per category. If you're unsure, budget generously. It's better to see a small green surplus than a misleading red that makes you think you've failed.

Concept

On Budget vs Off Budget: two kinds of accounts

Actual makes a distinction between accounts that affect your budget and accounts that are just tracked for your net worth. Understanding this is important before you add a single account.

✅ On Budget

Spending from these accounts is tracked against your budget categories.

  • Checking / Current account
  • Savings account
  • Credit cards
  • Cash wallet
📊 Off Budget

Tracked for net worth only. Transactions can't be categorized.

  • 401(k), IRA, HSA
  • Investment / brokerage
  • Mortgage, car loan
  • Property value
🚫
You cannot change an Off Budget account to On Budget later. Always double-check this setting — especially for credit cards, which must always be On Budget so their spending is tracked against your categories.

Adding your accounts

For each account, enter the current balance shown in your bank's app. Don't go back months — start from today. That balance becomes the opening transaction in Actual.

Concept

Transfers: moving money between your own accounts

When you move money between two accounts you own — checking to savings, or paying a credit card — you use a Transfer, not a regular categorized transaction. This matters because a transfer doesn't affect your budget: the money is still yours, it just moved.

In Actual this looks like…

In the Payee field, click Make Transfer and choose the destination account. Actual automatically creates matching entries in both accounts — a debit in one, a credit in the other. They're linked: editing one updates the other.

📍Click "Make Transfer" in the payee dropdown to see your accounts and choose the destination.
📍A completed transfer — both entries are linked. Updating the note in one automatically updates the other.
Concept

Reconciliation: verifying your records against your bank

Reconciliation is the habit of comparing Actual's transaction list to your official bank statement and confirming they match. It catches missing transactions, accidental duplicates, and amounts you may have mistyped.

In Actual this looks like…

Click the 🔒 lock icon in the top-right of any account. Enter your current bank balance. Actual shows you a "Difference" number. Go through transactions ticking the ones that appear on your statement (grey circle = unconfirmed, green = confirmed). When Difference reaches $0, click "Done Reconciling." Reconciled transactions are locked.

📍Click the green balance in the account header to see Cleared vs Uncleared totals — helps you know where the discrepancy is before you start reconciling.
💡
You don't need to reconcile every day. Once a month is fine for most people — just make sure you do it at least monthly if you import transactions manually.
Practical

Payees, tags, and bulk editing

Three features that make managing transactions much faster once you have real data coming in.

Payees — cleaning up ugly bank names

Banks give payees names like TARGET DEBIT CRD ACH TRAN CO ID:XXX15170. From More → Payees, you can rename them, merge duplicates, and set default categories. Actual also learns from your renaming and creates Rules automatically — so you only clean up each payee once.

📍More → Payees: search for all variations of a payee name to merge them into one clean entry.
📍After merging — all variations become one payee. Click the name to rename it to something clean like "Morrisons."

Tags — cross-category labels

Add #hashtags to any transaction's Notes field to group transactions that cross categories. Useful for tracking the real cost of a holiday (flights in Transport, hotels in Accommodation, meals in Eating Out — all tagged #spain2025).

📍Type # in the Notes field to add a tag — you'll see autocomplete for existing tags.
📍The Tags management page (sidebar) — assign colours to tags and view all transactions for any tag.

Bulk editing — update many transactions at once

Check the box on the left of multiple transactions, then use the toolbar to change category, payee, notes, account, or delete them all at once. Use Filters first to narrow down to exactly what you want to change.

⚠️
Bulk editing doesn't work well with split transactions. Avoid selecting split transactions when using bulk actions.
Practical

Filtering transactions

The Filter button above any transaction list lets you narrow what you see using multiple stacked conditions. This is far more powerful than text search — you can combine date ranges, categories, amounts, payees, and tags.

📍The Filter dropdown — choose from date, category, payee, amount, account, notes, and tags. Combine as many as you need.

Lesson 05 of 09 ⏱ 6 min

Credit Cards: The Part Everyone Gets Wrong

More questions are asked about credit cards in Actual than any other topic. This lesson explains the underlying concept first, then walks through a complete worked example so there are no surprises.

🎯 By the end of this lesson you will…
Understand how Actual treats credit cards (the core concept)
Know what happens to your budget when you swipe the card
Know how to record credit card payments correctly
Understand what to do if you carry a balance
Core Concept

The mental model: credit cards hold negative dollars

In Actual, a credit card is just another account — but it holds negative money. When you create the account, its balance is negative (what you currently owe). As you make purchases, the balance gets more negative. When you pay the card, positive dollars from your checking account cancel out the negative ones.

The most important thing to understand: your budget categories are updated at the moment of purchase, not when you pay the bill. When you buy $50 of groceries on your Visa, your Groceries category decreases by $50 immediately. Paying the card later is just accounting — moving money between accounts.

Why this is better than how you might be thinking about it

Some people think of credit card spending as "not real money yet" until the bill arrives. Actual disagrees — and rightly so. You've committed to that $50 the moment you bought the groceries. Recording it immediately means your budget always reflects reality, not a month-old picture.

Step by Step

Paying in full each month — the full workflow

1
Add your credit card as an On Budget account

Always On Budget — never Off Budget. Enter the current balance you owe (as a negative number).

2

3

4

✅
The golden rule: As long as you never spend more than your budget categories allow, you will always have enough money to pay your credit card in full.
Edge Case

If you carry a balance (paying minimum payments)

If you can't pay the full statement balance one month, the remaining negative balance on the card carries forward. To manage this in Tracking mode:

  • Create a category called "Visa Debt Payment" (or similar)
  • Budget the amount you plan to pay toward the card this month
  • Record the actual payment as a Transfer from Checking to the card
  • Increase the payment amount budget each month to accelerate payoff
💡
Interest charges from carrying a balance will appear as transactions on your card statement. Import them and categorize to a "Credit Card Interest" category so you can track what debt is actually costing you.

Lesson 07 of 09 ⏱ 5 min

Rules: Let Actual Do the Work For You

Manually categorizing every transaction gets old fast. Rules are how Actual learns from you and automates the repetitive work — so importing transactions eventually requires almost no effort.

🎯 By the end of this lesson you will…
Understand what rules are and how they work
Know how Actual creates rules automatically from your behaviour
Know how to view, edit, and create rules manually
Understand rule stages (pre, default, post)
Concept

What a rule is: an "if → then" instruction

Every time a transaction is imported, Actual runs it through all your rules in order. A rule has two parts: conditions (when to fire) and actions (what to change). If all conditions match, all actions are applied.

The most common use is cleaning up ugly payee names and setting categories automatically. For example:

If all these conditions are true…
Imported payeecontains"amazon"
Then apply these actions…
Set Payee→Amazon
Set Category→Shopping

This turns AMAZON.COM*5C7QC7MH0 AM 10/26 into "Amazon / Shopping" every single time — automatically.

The Good News

Actual writes most rules for you automatically

You don't need to build your rule library from scratch. Actual watches your behaviour and creates rules based on what you do:

A
When you rename a payee
Actual asks: "Apply this rename automatically in future?" Click yes and it creates a pre-stage rule. That ugly bank name will never appear again.
B
When you categorize a transaction
After you assign the same payee to a category a couple of times, Actual creates a rule to apply that category automatically going forward. After a few weeks of use, most transactions categorize themselves on import.
✨
The payoff: After a month or two of regular use, importing a week's worth of transactions takes about 2 minutes — Actual cleans up the names, assigns categories, and you just review and confirm. This is what makes the system sustainable long-term.

ConditionWhat it matchesExample

Conditions can check: imported payee, payee, account, category, date, notes, amount (inflow or outflow). All string matching is case-insensitive.

Actions can set: payee, category, notes, account, date, amount, cleared status. They can also append or prepend text to notes.

Practical

Rule stages: controlling the order

Rules run in three stages. Knowing this prevents confusion when multiple rules affect the same transaction.

pre
Pre-stage runs first, always. Actual automatically puts payee-renaming rules here. This ensures payee names are cleaned up before any categorization rules run.
default
Default-stage is where most rules live. Within this stage, more specific rules (using is) automatically rank after broader ones (using contains), so the specific rule always wins.
post
Post-stage runs last, always. Use this for rules that must override everything else. Most users never need this.
Action

Viewing and managing your rules

1
Go to More → Rules in the sidebar
See all your rules, including the ones Actual created automatically for you.
2
Click "edit" on any rule to view it in detail
The editor shows a live list of existing transactions that match your conditions — excellent feedback before saving.
3
Select transactions and click "Apply actions" to backfill
Retroactively apply a rule to past transactions — great for re-categorizing a batch of old records in one click.
💡
The rule editor is also a powerful bulk editor. Create a temporary rule with conditions that match what you want to change, apply actions to selected transactions, then delete the rule. Much faster than editing transactions one by one.

Report 1

Cash Flow: "Did I save money this month?"

Cash flow shows the balance of all your On Budget accounts over time. A month where the bar goes up means your accounts collectively grew — you saved money. A bar going down means you spent more than came in.

📍Cash Flow — green bars are saving months; red bars are spending months. A quick monthly health check.
How to use this report

In Tracking mode, compare cash flow against your Projected Savings. If Projected Savings said $550 but cash flow shows $200 growth, somewhere $350 went uncategorized or was undercounted. This nudges you to investigate.

Report 2

Net Worth: "Am I building wealth?"

Net worth is everything you own minus everything you owe — cash, investments, property equity, minus debts, mortgages, and loans. It's the single most important number in your financial life, and it should trend upward over time.

📍Net Worth over time — includes all accounts (On Budget and Off Budget). Add your mortgage and investment accounts as Off Budget accounts to see the full picture.
Report 3

Spending Analysis: "Where does my money actually go?"

This is the most revelatory report for Tracking mode users. It shows your actual spending broken down by category — not what you budgeted, but what you spent. Most people are surprised by at least one category.

📍Spending Analysis — your biggest categories and spending trends over time. This report tells you which categories to focus on.
How to use this report for better budgeting

After 2–3 months, look at this report with one question: "Which categories am I consistently spending more on than I budgeted?" Those are your high-signal categories. In Tracking mode, raise their budget targets to be more realistic. If you're ready for Envelope mode, those are the categories where firm limits will make the most difference to your savings.

Decision Framework

When are you ready to switch to Envelope mode?

Envelope mode isn't harder — it's just more demanding. It requires you to actively manage your budget mid-month, not just review it at the end. Here are the signs you're ready:

✅ You're probably ready if…
  • You've used Tracking mode for 2+ months
  • You know which categories you consistently overspend
  • You want overspending to require a conscious trade-off
  • You have a specific savings goal (emergency fund, holiday, debt payoff)
  • You check the app at least once a week
⏳ Stay in Tracking mode if…
  • You're still building the import/categorize habit
  • You forget to check the app for weeks at a time
  • You haven't yet had a full month of categorized data
  • The idea of "balancing to zero" feels stressful right now
📅
The ideal path: 2–3 months in Tracking mode → look at your Spending Analysis report → use those insights to set meaningful Envelope budgets → switch and experience noticeably better savings within 2 months. The data from Tracking mode makes your Envelope mode targets accurate, not guesswork.

FAQ

Common questions answered

Q: Do I have to categorize every single transaction?

A: Yes — in both modes, every transaction needs a category. Without categories, the reports have no meaning and the budget can't work. The good news: Rules handle most categorization automatically after a few weeks. Your weekly session will mostly be reviewing and confirming what Actual already did.

Q: In Tracking mode, do unused budget amounts carry over to next month?

A: No. This is a fundamental difference from Envelope mode. In Tracking mode, each month starts fresh — you set new targets each month (or copy last month's with one click). In Envelope mode, leftover money in categories rolls forward automatically and builds up over time.

Q: Can I sync my bank automatically?

A: Yes. SimpleFIN (US/Canada), GoCardless (EU/UK), and Pluggy.ai (Brazil) are supported. Set up your accounts with a correct opening balance first, then connect bank sync. Only transactions after the opening balance date are synced — which makes reconciliation straightforward.

Q: Is there a mobile app?

A: The dedicated mobile apps are no longer maintained. Use Actual in your phone's browser — the interface is responsive and works well. On iOS, tap Share → "Add to Home Screen" for a full-screen app experience. On Android, use "Add to Home Screen" from the browser menu.

Q: What if I overspend a category badly in Tracking mode?

A: Increase the budget number to clear the red and acknowledge reality. Your Projected Savings decreases accordingly — that's the honest picture of the month. Nothing is locked or broken. Next month, either set a more realistic target or decide to actively cut back in that category.

Q: How do I update Actual after a new version is released?

A: It depends on how you're hosting it. PikaPods auto-updates around a week after each release. Docker users pull the new image. Fly.io has a specific update guide in the official docs at actualbudget.org/docs.
